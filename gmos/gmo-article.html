GMOs:
Food That's Feared

Illustration by Hailey Usher

Written by Hailey Usher
Published on Monday, November 20, 2023

Genetically Modified Organisms (GMOs) harness the power of genetic engineering to enhance crop yields, bolster resistance to pests and diseases, and even improve nutritional content. GMOs’ potential to revolutionize agriculture and mitigate environmental issues makes them a compelling topic of research, yet also one of heated debate.

And So It Begins...

I’ve never been very interested in science; I haven't ever found a topic that intrigued me enough to conduct my own personal research outside of required school assignments. Despite my general apathy toward scientific research, I’ve somehow found myself being the most passionate and educated person I know on one particular topic: GMOs.

It all started one fateful day three years ago when I was a junior in high school, who naturally, was shopping for late-night snacks at Target. Being a selective eater, I wandered up and down the aisles, scrutinizing the labels on products to make sure I wasn’t buying anything with unhealthy ingredients. I saw the usual claims–all-natural, organic, made without dyes–and shrugged them off. But, the more I looked around, the more I noticed a new label type pop up: “GMO-free.” What the heck are GMOs? Are they bad? I wondered, not knowing the rabbit hole I was about to go down as my curiosity got the best of me, and I looked up the acronym on Google.

Flash forward to my sophomore year of college, and I have an arsenal of GMO knowledge and factoids that rivals that of a food scientist. Having conducted multiple research products on GMOs, as well as personal outside exploration, I’m practically a traveling salesman on the topic.

So why have I been so captivated by GMOs, you might ask? I’m fascinated by the sheer unlimited potential of the technology, yet also the staunch opinions people seem to have either for or against the process of genetic engineering. This polarization makes me curious about how we as a society have gotten to this point.

What are GMOs?

Grasping the science behind GMOs themselves is the crucial first step in understanding the puzzling contention surrounding the technology. So, let’s start there.

GMOs are organisms whose genetic makeup has been deliberately altered through a range of precise techniques. Essentially, these new technologies enable scientists to insert or delete specific genes to bring about desirable traits in an organism, such as built-in pest resistance or improved nutritional content.

The process of creating a GE (Genetically Engineered) organism typically begins with identifying the desired genetic trait, as mentioned above. To do this, scientists will isolate the gene responsible for this trait from another organism, which is often a completely unrelated species, and insert it into the DNA of the target organism.

A well-known example of a successful GE crop in today’s world is Golden Rice. Golden Rice is rice that has been genetically engineered to contain a high beta-carotene content, which is converted into Vitamin A by our bodies when consumed. This one simple improvement means that Golden Rice is inherently a more nutritious product than “regular” rice, which naturally does not contain beta-carotene.

Ultimately, the significance of Golden Rice lies in its combatting of Vitamin A Deficiency (VAD). The deficiency afflicts 250 million people worldwide, most of whom are women and children. As rice is a staple food in most Vitamin-A deficient communities, Golden Rice is a highly researched and effective solution to this worldwide health issue.

That being said, I was curious to learn about GMO-centric research being done at CU and found two professors who each studied GMOs through a different lens. I knew I had to take the opportunity to speak with both of them in person, and so that’s what I did.

Magic (GE) Beans!

First, let me introduce you to Biologist and Professor Brian DeDecker, who’s developing a GMO crop right here at CU. Professor DeDecker’s pioneering work delves into the potential of GMOs to not only address nutritional deficiencies but also reduce the environmental impact of conventional food production methods. I first found out about Professor DeDecker through a classmate at first and was immediately captivated by his work.

Through my interview with Professor DeDecker, I learned that one of the key challenges he tackles through his research at CU is to source essential proteins and nutrients to improve a certain food product via GE technology.

Baby formula, for instance, relies heavily on proteins obtained from cow's milk. While cow's milk serves as a staple for baby formula, it falls short in terms of nutritional content compared to human breast milk. Furthermore, the dairy industry is notorious for being unsustainable and resource-intensive. This also includes the significant greenhouse gas emissions resulting from methane, a chemical produced by cows' manure.

Within our interview, I learned of Professor DeDecker’s newest GE product, which bridges the nutritional gap between human-made and dairy-farmed breast milk. In a bold departure from traditional methods, Professor DeDecker has inserted molecular instructions for human breast milk into soybean plants, which he dubs, “magic beans.”

Although his decision to utilize a soybean plant to produce a breastmilk formula seemed strange at first to me, Professor DeDecker emphasized how soybeans are inherently eco-friendly crops and are even able to generate their own fertilizer. Professor DeDecker’s GE soybeans not only greatly reduce the need for synthetic products like fertilizer, thereby minimizing the biodiversity impact in specific cultivation areas, but are also a healthy and sustainable substitution to human breastmilk for babies that do not have access to human breastmilk.

When I mentioned the GMO debate to Professor DeDecker, curious to hear his opinion of it, he immediately highlighted the fundamental idea that all life on Earth is DNA-based, sharing an identical set of instructions and amino acids for life. This biological principle therefore enables the use of machinery for genetic alteration; at the molecular level, the transfer of DNA is seamlessly interpreted from organism to organism by these universal datasets.

Moreover, the use of GMOs introduces a shift away from the chemical additives that are found in most current crop production methods. Traditional agricultural practices commonly incorporate pesticides, whose molecules can be absorbed through the skin, potentially posing risks to human DNA as a result. GMOs, on the other hand, rely on the fundamental building blocks of life instead of these outside chemicals, which are inherently safe and even nutritious for our consumption.

Professor DeDecker also confronts the common Naturalism argument regarding GMOs head-on. While many argue that "natural" foods and processes are inherently superior, he reminds us that nature itself can, and is, harmful. For instance, snake venom is a natural substance, but it's far from beneficial. Professor DeDecker argues that every product, including GMOs, should be evaluated based on its own merits rather than preconceived notions of what is "natural."

In the context of the dairy industry, Professor DeDecker challenges us to reconsider our product choices. He points out the harsh realities of dairy farming, where cows often endure distressing, tortuous conditions. He even begs the question: “Would you prefer ‘natural’ cow's milk or a genetically modified, identical alternative produced by a plant?”

To wrap things up, Professor DeDecker recognizes that changing public perception about GMOs is a complex and gradual process. He believes the key to acceptance lies in the repetition of demonstrating the safety and effectiveness of genetic editing.

With every milestone achieved in his research here at CU, Professor DeDecker provides further evidence that GMOs are the safe and nutritious future of our agriculture industry. It’s exciting to think about what kind of products and solutions he will develop in the near future with GE technologies!

The Psychology Behind the Debate

Furthermore, the research led by Professor Philip Fernbach–a cognitive scientist and professor at the Leeds School of Business–sheds light on a particularly interesting paradox on the subject of GMOs. I was fascinated to read online about his psychological research on GMOs here at CU. Professor Fernbach recently coordinated a thorough study between researchers from the Leeds School of Business, Washington University in St. Louis, the University of Toronto, and the University of Pennsylvania; their work focused on the phenomenon of knowledge overconfidence in the context of GE foods.

The study's primary focus was to unravel the relationship between people's self-assessed knowledge about GE foods and their actual understanding of GMOs themselves. To achieve this, Professor Fernbach and the other researchers surveyed more than 2,000 adults from the U.S. and Europe. Respondents were first asked to rate their perceived level of knowledge about GMOs, and then their actual knowledge was assessed through a series of true-false questions related to general science and genetic editing.

In my discussion with Professor Fernbach, I discovered more about the outcome of the study. Essentially, the study uncovered a shocking paradoxical revelation, being that those who held the most extreme views opposing GMOs, believing they had a deep understanding of the topic, scored the lowest on the actual knowledge test. This striking result highlights the paradox of “knowledge overconfidence,” as Professor Fernbach labels it, which stays consistent with previous research that has been conducted on the psychology of extremism. This means that the GMO debate can be thought of similarly to that of climate change and even abortion.

Professor Fernbach’s study concludes that individuals who possess the least knowledge about vital scientific topics may remain in a state of unknowingness, which means that they are highly unlikely to actively seek out new information or be open to changing their viewpoints on GMOs. Professor Fernbach even solemnly comments on this issue at hand, saying, “I don’t think we’re on a pathway to those kinds of effects changing over time. If anything, they seem to be getting worse these days by people getting information through siloed communities on the Internet. I don’t see a lot of reason for optimism.” Without this initial recognition of their own knowledge gaps, educational interventions on GMOs will fail to align individuals with the scientific consensus on the topic.

What Can Be Done?!

I firmly believe that GMOs hold the key to transforming our approach to agriculture, nutrition, and sustainability in a world where our population grows at a rapid rate, and the effects of climate change continue to worsen.

While current forecasts on the state of the GMO debate remain bleak, and polarization on scientific topics remains steadfast, it’s up to us as educational individuals to keep pushing for accuracy in this environment.

So, while you don’t have to be a “traveling GMO salesman” like me, I encourage you to stay educated on this topic, ask questions, and be curious, just as I’ve done!
